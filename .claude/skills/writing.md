# Writing Skill - Point-First, Human-Sounding Report Writing

## When to Use
Use this skill when writing narrative text for reports, executive summaries, chart descriptions, key findings, section introductions, or any prose that accompanies data analysis. The goal: every paragraph sounds like a sharp analyst wrote it, not an AI.

## Core Principle: Point-First Writing

Every paragraph, bullet, and section leads with the conclusion. Context follows. The reader should get the headline from the first sentence alone.

### Bad (Context-First)
> "When examining the crime data across British Columbia from 2003 to 2023, taking into account various factors such as population growth and changes in reporting methodology, it becomes apparent that property crime rates have declined significantly."

### Good (Point-First)
> "Property crime rates fell 42% between 2003 and 2023 -- the steepest sustained drop in BC's recorded history. Population growth and improved reporting explain part of the decline, but not all of it."

## The Anti-AI Checklist

Run this checklist on every paragraph before finalizing. If any item is true, rewrite.

### Words and Phrases to Kill
These are AI-tell markers. Replace or delete every instance:

| Kill | Replace with |
|---|---|
| "It is worth noting that" | Delete entirely -- just state the thing |
| "It should be noted" | Delete entirely |
| "In order to" | "To" |
| "Utilize" | "Use" |
| "Leverage" | "Use" (or the specific verb: "apply", "exploit", "build on") |
| "Facilitate" | The specific action it enables |
| "Comprehensive" | Delete or specify what's included |
| "Robust" | Delete or specify what makes it strong |
| "Delve into" | "Examine", "look at", "break down" |
| "Landscape" (non-geographic) | "situation", "picture", "state of" |
| "Tapestry" | Never |
| "Multifaceted" | Delete or list the actual facets |
| "Furthermore" | Delete (start the next sentence with the point) |
| "Moreover" | Delete |
| "Additionally" | Delete or use "Also" |
| "In conclusion" | Delete (the conclusion should be obvious) |
| "This suggests that" | State the conclusion directly |
| "It is important to" | Delete -- if it's important, the reader will see why |
| "Significant" (without a p-value) | Specify: "large", "meaningful", "a 40% increase" |
| "Various" | Name them or give a count |
| "Overall" | Delete or replace with the specific scope |

### Structural Anti-Patterns
- **Throat-clearing openers**: "In this section, we will examine..." -- delete, just examine it
- **Hedge stacking**: "It might potentially be somewhat likely that..." -- pick one hedge or none
- **Passive voice by default**: "The data was analyzed" -- "We analyzed the data" or "The analysis shows"
- **List-everything syndrome**: Don't list every data point. Pick the 2-3 that matter most and contextualize them
- **Missing "so what"**: Every statistic needs a reason to exist. "Crime rose 15%" is incomplete. "Crime rose 15%, reversing a decade-long decline" tells the reader why they should care

## Verb Intensity Ladder

Match verb strength to the magnitude of change. Weak verbs for small changes, strong verbs for dramatic ones.

| Magnitude | Verbs |
|---|---|
| < 5% change | edged up, dipped, held steady, inched, nudged |
| 5-15% change | rose, fell, grew, declined, climbed, slipped |
| 15-30% change | jumped, dropped, surged, sank, accelerated |
| 30-50% change | soared, plunged, spiked, collapsed, rocketed |
| > 50% change | exploded, cratered, doubled, halved, transformed |

### Examples
- 3% increase: "Property crime **edged up** 3% year-over-year"
- 18% decrease: "Violent crime **dropped** 18% following the policy change"
- 58% increase: "Homicides **spiked** 58% between 2019 and 2020"

## Three Forms Rule

Present key numbers in three forms so every reader finds one that clicks:

1. **Percentage**: "a 42% decline"
2. **Absolute**: "from 85,000 to 49,000 incidents"
3. **Comparison**: "roughly equivalent to the entire caseload of Surrey disappearing"

You don't need all three every time, but important numbers deserve at least two.

## Paragraph Templates

### Finding Paragraph
> [What happened] + [how much] + [why it matters].
> [Supporting detail or exception]. [Implication or next question].

Example:
> "Homicides spiked 58% between 2019 and 2021, from 12 to 19 per year. Every other violent crime category declined or held flat during the same period, making homicide an outlier. Whether this reflects a genuine shift in severity or a reporting artifact warrants further investigation."

### Chart Description
> [Headline finding from the chart]. [The pattern or trend]. [The exception or surprise]. [Why the reader should look at it].

Example:
> "The Central Business District dominates crime volume so completely that it compresses all other neighbourhoods into visual irrelevance. After excluding CBD, a clear gradient emerges: Grandview-Woodland and Mount Pleasant lead the remaining neighbourhoods, at roughly triple the count of quieter areas like Shaughnessy. The chart repays close reading -- several neighbourhoods shifted rank significantly between 2018 and 2023."

### Executive Summary Bullet
> [What] + [how much] + [so what]

Example:
> "Property crime fell 42% (85k to 49k incidents), the steepest two-decade decline on record, suggesting structural deterrence rather than cyclical fluctuation."

## Hedge Calibration

### Budget: One hedge per claim
Not: "It might potentially suggest that crime could possibly be declining"
But: "Crime appears to be declining" (one hedge: "appears")

### Proportioning: 70/20/10
- 70% of claims: **confident** -- state directly, no hedge
- 20% of claims: **light hedge** -- "likely", "appears", "suggests"
- 10% of claims: **strong hedge** -- "may", "might", "it is unclear whether"

If you're hedging more than 30% of your claims, you're either uncertain about your data (fix the data) or defaulting to caution out of habit (stop).

## 8-Pass Revision Protocol

Run these passes sequentially on any report text before finalizing:

1. **Point-first pass**: Move the conclusion to the first sentence of every paragraph
2. **Filler pass**: Delete every instance of the kill-list words above
3. **Verb pass**: Check every verb against the intensity ladder -- does the verb match the magnitude?
4. **Hedge pass**: Count hedges per paragraph. Max one. Delete extras
5. **Passive pass**: Convert passive voice to active unless passive is genuinely clearer
6. **So-what pass**: Every statistic must answer "why should the reader care?"
7. **Read-aloud pass**: Read each paragraph aloud (or mentally). If you stumble, the sentence is too long or too tangled. Split it
8. **AI-tell pass**: Final scan for anything that sounds like ChatGPT wrote it. Be ruthless

## Tone Targets

### Aim for
- Confident but not arrogant
- Specific, not vague
- Direct, not brusque
- Analytical, not academic
- Engaged, not breathless

### The "analyst at a whiteboard" test
Imagine explaining this finding to a smart colleague at a whiteboard. You wouldn't say "It is worth noting that the data reveals a comprehensive landscape of multifaceted crime trends." You'd say "Property crime is down 42% -- that's huge. But homicides spiked in 2020 and haven't come back down. Something changed."

Write like that.

## Data Narration Patterns

### Contextualizing a number
- **Temporal**: "the highest since 2008", "reversing a 5-year trend"
- **Relative**: "3x the provincial average", "half the rate of neighbouring jurisdictions"
- **Tangible**: "equivalent to one incident per 200 residents", "roughly one per day"

### Describing trends
Don't: "The trend shows an increase over time."
Do: "Crime climbed steadily from 2003 to 2015, plateaued for three years, then fell sharply after 2018 -- a pattern consistent with the opioid-driven enforcement shift."

### Calling out exceptions
The most interesting finding is usually the thing that breaks the pattern. Lead with it:
"Every crime category declined between 2015 and 2023 except one: cyber fraud tripled."
