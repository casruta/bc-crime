# British Columbia Crime Analysis: Summary of Findings

## Overview

This analysis examines crime trends across British Columbia using Statistics Canada open data (1998-2024), BC Government justice appendices (2014-2023), and consumer price index data. It covers five research questions through 41 publication-ready charts, 8+ formatted tables, and one interactive map.

## 1. Is Crime Rising?

BC's Crime Severity Index peaked at 166.9 in 2003, then fell 46% to 90.2 by 2014. Since 2014, CSI has risen modestly but remains well below the historical peak. The latest year (2024) brought a 7.4% decline, the second-largest drop among comparison provinces after Alberta (-8.5%).

BC consistently exceeds the national average crime rate, sitting roughly 21% above it in the latest year. All provinces share the same broad pattern: a peak around 2003, a sustained decline to 2014, then a plateau or slight rise.

A compositional shift is underway. Violent CSI has risen more steeply than non-violent CSI since 2014, meaning the mix of crime is tilting toward more serious offences even as the overall index stabilizes.

## 2. What Kinds of Crime Are Changing?

Property crime represents 51% of the total crime rate but has been steadily declining since the early 2000s. The largest absolute declines over five years include theft from vehicles (-772 per 100,000), breaking and entering (-270), and theft under $5,000 (-221).

On the other side, child pornography offences (+82 per 100,000) and shoplifting (+79) show the largest absolute increases. Violent crime's share of total crime is growing proportionally even though its absolute rate remains relatively flat.

The analysis tracks compound annual growth rates by category, violation-level heatmaps, and slope charts ranking each violation's trajectory over time.

## 3. How Effectively Is Crime Being Addressed?

Clearance rates vary dramatically by crime type. Violent crime clears above 50%, the highest among categories. Property crime clears below 30%, the lowest. These rates have remained relatively stable across the 2004-2024 period.

Youth crime severity has continued to decline since 2014 while adult CSI has risen or stabilized, suggesting youth-specific interventions or demographic shifts are at work.

Unfounded rates (the share of reports determined to be non-criminal) have remained stable over time, ruling out changes in police classification as a major driver of the apparent crime decline.

Underreporting data from the 2019 General Social Survey reveals that only 29% of criminal victimizations are reported to police nationally. Reporting rates range from 6% for sexual assault to 60% for motor vehicle theft. Property crime, which has a higher reporting rate, declined more steeply than violent crime, which has a lower reporting rate. This is the opposite of what a reporting-driven decline would produce.

### Policing Costs

BC police spending has risen steadily in both nominal and inflation-adjusted terms, even as crime severity fell. Per-capita policing costs are tracked against comparison provinces. Staffing levels (officers per 100,000) are measured against population growth, and a crimes-per-officer workload metric is overlaid with CSI to assess whether declining crime translated to reduced workload.

## 4. Where Is Crime Concentrated?

Vancouver (~48,812 offences) and Surrey (~41,275) dominate BC's total crime count. But per-capita rates tell a different story. Interior and smaller CMAs have dramatically higher rates:

- Chilliwack: 11,352 per 100,000 (roughly 2x Vancouver)
- Kamloops: 10,546 per 100,000
- Vancouver: 5,438 per 100,000
- Victoria: 5,283 per 100,000

Economic conditions, homelessness prevalence, substance use patterns, and policing model differences drive this urban-rural divide. A composite neighbourhood ranking combining volume (60% weight) and trend direction (40%) classifies areas as high, moderate, or lower concern, identifying places that are both high-crime and worsening.

## 5. Why Does Crime Feel Like It's Rising?

Public perception significantly lags reality. In 2019, 42% of BC residents believed crime had increased in their neighbourhood, up from 30% in 2014, even as the CSI remained below its historical peak.

From 2009-2014, perception and reality aligned: crime fell, and fewer people believed it was rising. From 2014-2019, perception and reality diverged: crime stabilized or slightly rose, but the perception of increase jumped sharply.

Confidence in police is moderate: 30% of BC residents reported a "great deal of confidence" in 2019, with 47% reporting "some confidence." Reporting rate variability (from 6% for sexual assault to 60% for motor vehicle theft) means changes in reporting practices can create perceived changes independent of actual crime frequency.

## Key Implications

Five findings from this analysis demand attention:

1. **The violent shift matters more than the headline number.** BC's overall CSI fell 7.4% in 2024, but violent CSI has been climbing since 2014 while non-violent CSI drives the decline. Policy that celebrates the headline drop without addressing the violent-crime crossover risks misallocating resources toward property crime reduction that's already happening organically.

2. **Interior communities need disproportionate support.** Chilliwack and Kamloops face per-capita crime rates roughly 2x Vancouver's, yet public discourse and resource allocation skew toward Lower Mainland concerns. The composite ranking (volume + trend) identifies specific areas that are both high-crime and worsening — those should top the priority list.

3. **Property crime clearance is a systemic failure.** Below 30% clearance for property offences means 7 in 10 go unresolved. With theft from vehicles declining by 772 per 100,000 over five years, the drop may owe more to changing behaviour (fewer valuables left visible, more secure vehicles) than to enforcement. This raises the question of whether property crime investigation resources should be redeployed toward the rising violent categories.

4. **The perception gap is a governance problem.** When 42% of residents believe crime is rising while the data shows a decline, trust in institutions erodes regardless of performance. Transparent, regular publication of CSI trends (not just incident counts) and visible community policing in the most-affected interior areas could narrow this gap.

5. **Child exploitation and shoplifting trends require distinct responses.** These two categories lead absolute increases but for entirely different reasons. Child exploitation (+82/100k) reflects expanded digital investigation capacity and mandatory reporting — a detection increase, not necessarily a prevalence increase. Shoplifting (+79/100k) may reflect genuine behavioural change, reduced enforcement, or changed reporting thresholds. Conflating them in policy would be a mistake.

## Methodology & Limitations

The analysis draws on six Statistics Canada tables and four BC Government appendices. Key metrics include the Crime Severity Index (weighted by average sentence severity, base year 2006), crime rate per 100,000 population, clearance rates, and unfounded rates.

Limitations include reliance on police-reported data only (the "dark figure" of unreported crime is not captured), the COVID-19 anomaly distorting 2020-2021 data, clearance not equalling conviction, and reporting rate variability creating blind spots across crime types.
