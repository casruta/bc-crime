# British Columbia Crime Analysis: Summary of Findings

**Crime in BC is falling in volume but shifting in character.** The Crime Severity Index dropped 46% from its 2003 peak of 166.9 to 90.2 in 2014, then fell another 7.4% in the latest year (2024). Yet violent crime's share has been climbing since 2014 while property crime collapses beneath it. Interior communities like Chilliwack (11,352 per 100,000) face crime rates double Vancouver's (5,438). And 42% of BC residents believe crime is rising despite two decades of decline. This summary distils five research questions, 41 charts, and 20 years of Statistics Canada data into the findings that matter most.

---

## 1. Crime Is Falling — but the Mix Is Getting Worse

BC's Crime Severity Index peaked at 166.9 in 2003, fell 46% to 90.2 by 2014, and dropped a further 7.4% in 2024 — the second-largest provincial decline after Alberta (-8.5%). Over the full series, BC recorded 18 years of declining crime rates against 8 years of increases. The headline trend is unmistakably downward, and linear regression confirms the decline is statistically significant (p < 0.05).

The composition tells a different story. Violent CSI has risen more steeply than non-violent CSI since 2014: the overall index stabilized because property crime's decline masked a violent-crime crossover. BC still sits roughly 21% above the national average. All provinces share the same arc — peak around 2003, decline to 2014, plateau since — but Saskatchewan runs at roughly 2x Ontario's rate, and BC's gap with the national average has persisted across the entire series.

Indexed to 2004 = 100, every comparison province (Alberta, Ontario, Saskatchewan, Manitoba) shows a converging downward trajectory, with BC tracking the middle of the pack. The pattern is structural, not provincial.

## 2. Property Crime Is Collapsing; Two Categories Are Surging

Property crime accounts for 51% of the total rate but has been declining steadily since the early 2000s. The five-year absolute drops are steep:

| Violation | 5-Year Change (per 100,000) |
|---|---|
| Theft from vehicles | -772 |
| Breaking and entering | -270 |
| Theft under $5,000 | -221 |
| Disturb the peace | -165 |

Two categories bucked the trend. Child pornography offences rose by 82 per 100,000 and shoplifting by 79. These require distinct interpretation: the child exploitation increase likely reflects expanded digital investigation and mandatory reporting (a detection gain, not necessarily a prevalence gain), while the shoplifting rise may reflect genuine behavioural change, reduced enforcement, or shifted reporting thresholds.

Violent crime's absolute rate remains relatively flat, but its share of total crime is growing as property crime falls away beneath it. A Pareto analysis shows that a small cluster of crime types accounts for roughly 80% of total volume. The chi-squared test for crime-type composition across years is statistically significant (p < 0.05), confirming the shift is real, not noise.

## 3. The Justice System Clears Violent Crime but Loses Property Crime

Clearance rates split sharply by type. Violent crime clears above 50%; property crime clears below 30% — meaning 7 in 10 property crimes go unresolved. These rates have held steady over the 2004-2024 period, a structural gap rather than a temporary shortfall.

Youth crime severity has diverged from the adult trend, continuing to decline since 2014 while adult CSI rose or stabilized. Whatever is driving youth crime downward — demographic shifts, intervention programs, or both — it operates independently of the forces pushing adult crime up.

The data rules out two alternative explanations for the overall decline. Unfounded rates (reports classified as non-criminal) have remained stable, so the drop isn't police reclassifying incidents. And the underreporting pattern runs the wrong way: property crime (reported to police ~35% of the time) declined more steeply than violent crime (~24% reported). A reporting-driven decline would produce the opposite pattern. The 2019 General Social Survey puts overall reporting at just 29% nationally, ranging from 6% for sexual assault to 60% for motor vehicle theft.

### Policing costs are rising faster than crime is falling

BC police spending grew in both nominal and inflation-adjusted (constant 2020 dollar) terms even as crime severity dropped. Salary and benefits growth outpaced operating and capital spending. BC's population grew 10.4% from 5,000,879 (2018) to 5,519,913 (2023), raising the question of whether staffing kept pace. The crimes-per-officer workload metric, overlaid with CSI, suggests declining crime volume was absorbed by expanding non-crime demands — mental health response, social disorder calls, technology investment — rather than translating into reduced workload.

## 4. Crime Concentrates in the Interior, Not the Lower Mainland

Vancouver (~48,812 offences) and Surrey (~41,275) lead in absolute volume, but per-capita rates reverse the map:

| Community | Rate per 100,000 | vs. Vancouver |
|---|---|---|
| Chilliwack | 11,352 | 2.1x |
| Kamloops | 10,546 | 1.9x |
| Vancouver | 5,438 | 1.0x |
| Victoria | 5,283 | 0.97x |

Economic conditions, homelessness prevalence, substance use, and policing model differences (municipal forces vs. RCMP detachments) drive this divide.

A composite neighbourhood ranking (60% standardized volume, 40% trend direction) classifies areas as high, moderate, or lower concern — identifying places that are both high-crime and worsening. An ANOVA variance decomposition partitions total crime variation across year, crime type, and neighbourhood, revealing which dimension explains the most variance and therefore which policy lever will have the largest effect.

## 5. The Public Thinks Crime Is Rising — the Data Disagrees

The perception-reality gap widened between 2014 and 2019:

| Year | "Crime increased" | "About the same" |
|---|---|---|
| 2009 | 38% | 43% |
| 2014 | 30% | 48% |
| 2019 | 42% | 41% |

From 2009 to 2014, perception tracked reality: crime fell and fewer people believed it was rising. From 2014 to 2019, crime stabilized or rose slightly while perceived increase jumped 12 percentage points — a disproportionate response that likely reflects media framing, high-profile incidents, and visible social disorder more than the statistical trend.

Confidence in police is middling. In 2019, 30% of BC residents reported "a great deal of confidence," 47% "some confidence," 17% "not very much," and 5% "none at all" — closely mirroring the national distribution (32%/46%/15%/5%).

Reporting rate variability compounds the gap. When only 6% of sexual assaults reach police but 60% of motor vehicle thefts do, the public's lived experience of crime diverges from the statistical record in ways that data publication alone won't bridge.

## What These Findings Mean — and What to Do About Them

### Priority 1: Confront the violent-crime crossover

The 7.4% CSI drop in 2024 is welcome, but it masks a compositional shift. Violent CSI has climbed since 2014 while property-crime decline drives the headline number down. The risk: declaring victory on a trend that's solving itself while the worsening trend goes unaddressed.

**Next steps:** Track violent and non-violent CSI separately in quarterly reporting. Flag any quarter where the violent-to-total ratio exceeds the previous year's annual average. Allocate new investigative resources proportional to the violent-crime share trend, not the total-crime level.

### Priority 2: Rebalance resources toward interior communities

Chilliwack (11,352/100k) and Kamloops (10,546/100k) face per-capita rates roughly 2x Vancouver's (5,438/100k), yet public discourse and provincial funding skew toward the Lower Mainland. The composite ranking identifies areas that are both high-crime and worsening — these are the places where additional officers, prevention programs, and social services will yield the largest marginal return.

**Next steps:** Use the composite ranking (volume 60% + trend 40%) as input to provincial resource allocation formulas. Conduct root-cause assessments in the top-5 ranked communities, focusing on the socioeconomic drivers (homelessness, substance use, economic precarity) that differentiate them from lower-rate coastal areas.

### Priority 3: Redeploy property-crime investigation capacity

Property crime clears below 30% — 7 in 10 cases unresolved — and the category is declining steeply anyway (theft from vehicles: -772/100k over five years). Violent crime clears above 50%, meaning additional investigative effort in that category is more likely to produce a solved case.

**Next steps:** Audit current detective assignment by crime category. Model the clearance-rate gain from shifting one FTE from property to violent crime investigation. Pilot in one high-composite-rank interior jurisdiction and measure clearance outcomes over 12 months.

### Priority 4: Close the perception-reality gap

The 12-point jump in perceived crime increase (30% to 42%, 2014-2019) occurred while CSI remained below its historical peak. Unchecked, this gap erodes institutional trust regardless of operational performance.

**Next steps:** Publish a quarterly, plain-language CSI dashboard at the provincial level — covering both total and violent/non-violent breakdowns, and highlighting per-capita rates by community. Pair with community policing visibility in the 10 highest-rate jurisdictions. Target: reduce the "crime increased" perception in the next GSS cycle (2024 or 2029) by at least 5 percentage points.

### Priority 5: Separate detection increases from prevalence increases

Child exploitation offences (+82/100k) and shoplifting (+79/100k) lead absolute increases but for opposite reasons. Expanded digital investigation capacity and mandatory reporting drove the first; behavioural or enforcement changes likely drove the second. Treating both as "crime waves" leads to misallocation in both directions.

**Next steps:** For child exploitation, track case-initiation source (proactive tip vs. victim report) to confirm the detection-gain hypothesis. For shoplifting, compare incident reports against retail loss surveys and enforcement-hour data to isolate which factor — actual theft, reporting threshold changes, or reduced deterrence — is the primary driver.

## Methodology & Limitations

**Data sources:** Six Statistics Canada tables (35-10-0063-01, 35-10-0177-01, 18-10-0005-01, 35-10-0076-01, 35-10-0059-01, 35-10-0066/0068-01) and four BC Government justice appendices (F-I). BC population estimates from BC Stats (2018: 5,000,879 to 2023: 5,519,913).

**Key metrics:** Crime Severity Index (sentence-severity weighted, base 2006), crime rate per 100,000, clearance rate, unfounded rate, CAGR, chi-squared composition test, ANOVA variance decomposition.

**Statistical methods:** Linear regression with 95% confidence intervals on slope, chi-squared test for compositional independence, Ward's hierarchical clustering for neighbourhood grouping, ANOVA for variance partitioning, 2-year trend projections with fan-chart uncertainty bands.

**Limitations:** Police-reported data only — the "dark figure" of unreported crime (71% nationally per 2019 GSS) is not captured. COVID-19 distorts 2020-2021. Clearance does not equal conviction. Per-capita rates use mid-year population estimates revised post-census. Reporting rates range from 6% (sexual assault) to 60% (motor vehicle theft), creating structural blind spots that no methodology can fully resolve.
