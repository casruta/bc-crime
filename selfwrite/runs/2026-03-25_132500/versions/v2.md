# British Columbia Crime Analysis: Summary of Findings

**Crime in BC is falling in volume but shifting in character.** The Crime Severity Index dropped 46% from its 2003 peak to 2014 and fell another 7.4% in 2024, yet violent crime's share has been climbing since 2014. Interior communities face per-capita rates double those of Vancouver, and 42% of BC residents believe crime is rising despite the data showing otherwise. This summary distils five research questions, 41 charts, and 20 years of Statistics Canada data into the findings that matter most.

---

## 1. Crime Is Falling — but the Mix Is Getting Worse

BC's Crime Severity Index peaked at 166.9 in 2003, fell 46% to 90.2 by 2014, and dropped a further 7.4% in 2024 — the second-largest provincial decline after Alberta (-8.5%). The headline trend is unmistakably downward.

The composition tells a different story. Violent CSI has risen more steeply than non-violent CSI since 2014: the overall index stabilized because property crime's decline masked a violent-crime crossover. BC still sits roughly 21% above the national average. All provinces share the same arc — peak around 2003, decline to 2014, plateau since — but BC's gap with the national average has persisted.

Linear trend analysis confirms the decline is statistically significant (p < 0.05), though the model doesn't capture the non-linear COVID-19 disruption in 2020-2021.

## 2. Property Crime Is Collapsing; Two Categories Are Surging

Property crime represents 51% of the total rate but has been declining steadily since the early 2000s. The five-year drops are steep: theft from vehicles fell by 772 per 100,000, breaking and entering by 270, theft under $5,000 by 221.

Two categories are moving the other direction. Child pornography offences rose by 82 per 100,000 and shoplifting by 79. These require distinct interpretation: the child exploitation increase likely reflects expanded digital investigation and mandatory reporting (a detection gain, not necessarily a prevalence gain), while the shoplifting rise may reflect genuine behavioural change, reduced enforcement, or shifted reporting thresholds.

Violent crime's absolute rate remains relatively flat, but its share of total crime is growing as property crime falls away beneath it. A Pareto analysis shows a small number of crime types account for the majority of volume — useful for resource prioritization.

## 3. The Justice System Clears Violent Crime but Loses Property Crime

Clearance rates split sharply by type. Violent crime clears above 50%; property crime clears below 30%. These rates have held steady over the 2004-2024 period, suggesting a structural gap rather than a temporary shortfall.

Youth crime severity has diverged from the adult trend, continuing to decline since 2014 while adult CSI rose or stabilized. This points to youth-specific interventions or demographic shifts working independently of the broader trend.

The data rules out two alternative explanations for the crime decline. Unfounded rates (reports classified as non-criminal) have remained stable, so the drop isn't police reclassifying reports. And the underreporting pattern goes the wrong way: property crime (35% reported to police) declined more steeply than violent crime (24% reported). A reporting-driven decline would produce the opposite pattern.

### Policing costs are rising faster than crime is falling

BC police spending has grown in both nominal and real terms even as crime severity dropped. Salary and benefits growth outpaced operating and capital spending. The crimes-per-officer workload metric, overlaid with CSI, raises the question of whether declining crime volume has been absorbed by expanding non-crime demands (mental health, social disorder) rather than translating into reduced workload.

## 4. Crime Concentrates in the Interior, Not the Lower Mainland

Vancouver (~48,812 offences) and Surrey (~41,275) lead in absolute volume, but per-capita rates reverse the map. Interior and smaller CMAs face dramatically higher crime intensity:

| Community | Rate per 100,000 | vs. Vancouver |
|---|---|---|
| Chilliwack | 11,352 | 2.1x |
| Kamloops | 10,546 | 1.9x |
| Vancouver | 5,438 | 1.0x |
| Victoria | 5,283 | 0.97x |

Economic conditions, homelessness prevalence, substance use, and policing model differences drive this divide. A composite ranking (60% volume, 40% trend direction) identifies areas that are both high-crime and worsening — the places that should top any resource allocation list.

An ANOVA variance decomposition reveals how much of the variation in crime is explained by geography vs. crime type vs. time, informing whether interventions should be place-based, type-based, or period-based.

## 5. The Public Thinks Crime Is Rising — the Data Disagrees

The perception-reality gap is widening. In 2019, 42% of BC residents believed crime had increased in their neighbourhood, up from 30% in 2014. The actual CSI was below its historical peak in both years.

The alignment broke after 2014. From 2009 to 2014, perception tracked reality: crime fell, and fewer people thought it was rising. From 2014 to 2019, crime stabilized or rose slightly while perception of increase jumped by 12 percentage points — a disproportionate response.

Confidence in police is middling: 30% of BC residents reported a "great deal of confidence" (2019 GSS), with 47% saying "some." Reporting rate variability compounds the problem. When only 6% of sexual assaults reach police but 60% of motor vehicle thefts do, the public's experience of crime differs from the statistical record in ways that no amount of data publication will fully bridge.

---

## Key Implications

Five findings demand action:

1. **Address the violent shift, not just the headline decline.** The 7.4% CSI drop in 2024 is welcome, but violent crime has been climbing since 2014 while property crime drives the decline. Policy that celebrates the headline without confronting the compositional shift risks misallocating resources toward a problem that's solving itself.

2. **Redirect resources to interior communities.** Chilliwack and Kamloops face per-capita rates 2x Vancouver's, yet discourse and funding skew toward the Lower Mainland. The composite ranking identifies specific areas that are both high-crime and worsening — those should top the priority list.

3. **Rethink property crime investigation.** Below-30% clearance means 7 in 10 property crimes go unresolved. The steep decline in theft from vehicles (-772/100k) likely owes more to changed behaviour than enforcement. Investigation resources could be redeployed toward rising violent categories where clearance rates are already higher and marginal effort yields more impact.

4. **Close the perception gap with transparent data.** When 42% of residents think crime is rising while it isn't, institutional trust erodes regardless of performance. Regular, accessible CSI reporting — not just incident counts — and visible policing in high-rate interior communities could narrow this gap.

5. **Distinguish detection gains from prevalence gains.** Child exploitation offences (+82/100k) and shoplifting (+79/100k) lead absolute increases but for opposite reasons. Treating expanded detection capacity as a crime wave, or a genuine behavioural shift as a reporting artefact, leads to misguided policy in both cases.

## Methodology & Limitations

**Data sources:** Six Statistics Canada tables (35-10-0063-01, 35-10-0177-01, 18-10-0005-01, 35-10-0076-01, 35-10-0059-01, 35-10-0066/0068-01) and four BC Government justice appendices (F-I).

**Key metrics:** Crime Severity Index (sentence-severity weighted, base 2006), crime rate per 100,000, clearance rate, unfounded rate, CAGR.

**Limitations:** Police-reported data only (unreported crime excluded). COVID-19 distorts 2020-2021. Clearance does not equal conviction. Per-capita rates use mid-year population estimates revised post-census. Reporting rates vary from 6% (sexual assault) to 60% (motor vehicle theft), creating structural blind spots.
