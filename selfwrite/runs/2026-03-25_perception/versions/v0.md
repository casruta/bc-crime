## 5. The Public Thinks Crime Is Rising — the Data Disagrees

The data is clear: BC's Crime Severity Index fell 46% from 2003 to 2014 and dropped another 7.4% in 2024. Public perception tells a different story. In the 2019 General Social Survey, 42% of BC residents said crime in their neighbourhood had increased — up from 30% in 2014.

| Year | "Crime increased" | "About the same" |
|---|---|---|
| 2009 | 38% | 43% |
| 2014 | 30% | 48% |
| 2019 | 42% | 41% |

Figure 31 overlays the perception data against actual CSI, making the divergence visible.

![Figure 31: Perception vs. reality — 42% believe crime is rising while CSI remains below its peak](outputs/charts/q5_perception_vs_reality.png)

The perception is wrong. But it is wrong for identifiable, correctable reasons — not because the public is irrational. Five mechanisms drive the gap, each traceable to specific data in this analysis.

### Why perception diverges from reality

**1. Visible crimes are rising; the crimes driving the decline are invisible.** Shoplifting increased by 79 per 100,000 over five years (Figure 16). It happens in grocery stores, pharmacies, and transit stations — places people visit daily. The crimes behind the aggregate decline are non-events: a car that wasn't broken into (theft from vehicles: -772/100k, Figure 12), a house that wasn't burglarized. The public sees the increases and misses the far larger decreases because declining crimes produce no observable signal.

**2. Violent crime IS rising — the headline number hides it.** Violent CSI has climbed since 2014 while non-violent CSI has fallen (Figure 2). The overall index declines because property crime's massive drop masks the violent crossover. Residents who sense that crime is "getting worse" are responding to a real signal — the shift toward more serious offences. The aggregate CSI obscures this by weighting all categories into a single number.

**3. Low clearance rates undermine statistical credibility.** Property crime clears below 30% — 7 in 10 cases go unresolved (Figures 17-18). A resident who reports a theft and receives no follow-up has no reason to trust the system's claim that crime is falling. Low clearance doesn't just fail to resolve cases; it erodes public confidence in the institution producing the statistics.

**4. Interior communities face genuinely high crime.** Chilliwack (11,352/100k) and Kamloops (10,546/100k) have per-capita rates roughly 2x Vancouver's 5,438 (Figure 26). For residents of these communities, the perception that crime is high matches their local reality. The provincial GSS averages their experience with lower-crime coastal populations, producing a misleading aggregate.

**5. Visible disorder registers as crime even when it isn't.** Open drug use, encampments, and aggressive panhandling are not Criminal Code offences captured in the CSI. But residents in communities affected by the opioid crisis and housing precarity experience these conditions daily. The gap between what people see (disorder) and what the statistics measure (Criminal Code offences) is structural — no amount of data publication will bridge it without addressing the underlying conditions.

### Confidence and reporting rates reinforce the gap

![Figure 32: Confidence in police by province — BC's middling confidence mirrors the national pattern](outputs/charts/q5_confidence_by_province.png)

Police confidence is moderate: 30% of BC residents report "a great deal of confidence," 47% report "some," 17% "not very much," and 5% "none at all" (2019 GSS). These figures mirror the national distribution (32%/46%/15%/5%). Low confidence compounds the perception problem — residents who don't trust police don't trust police-reported statistics.

![Figure 33: Reporting rates by crime type — from 6% (sexual assault) to 60% (motor vehicle theft)](outputs/charts/q5_reporting_rates.png)

Reporting rates vary from 6% for sexual assault to 60% for motor vehicle theft (Figure 33). Nationally, 71% of victimizations go unreported (2019 GSS). The statistical record captures a filtered subset of criminal activity. Victims of unreported crimes know their experience is real regardless of whether it appears in the data.

### Corrective actions

The perception gap is an information problem with operational solutions:

- **Disaggregate reporting.** Publish violent and non-violent CSI separately. The public intuits the violent crossover; confirming it rebuilds statistical credibility.
- **Improve clearance communication.** When a case is cleared, notify the reporting victim. When it isn't, explain why. Silence after a report fuels distrust.
- **Localize the data.** Provincial aggregates mask interior communities' reality. Publish per-capita rates by jurisdiction so residents see their own community reflected, not a provincial average driven by Vancouver.
- **Distinguish crime from disorder.** Visible disorder drives perception but falls outside the CSI. Track and report disorder-related calls separately so the public discourse doesn't conflate encampments with Criminal Code offences.
