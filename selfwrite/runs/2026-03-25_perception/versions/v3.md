## 5. The Public Thinks Crime Is Rising — the Data Disagrees

Crime in BC is falling. The Crime Severity Index dropped 46% from 2003 to 2014 and another 7.4% in 2024. These are not estimates or projections — they are police-reported, sentence-weighted measures covering every Criminal Code offence in the province. The decline is statistically significant (p < 0.05) and consistent across 18 of the last 26 years.

The public does not believe this. In 2019, 42% of BC residents told the General Social Survey that crime in their neighbourhood had increased — up 12 points from 30% in 2014. That perception is factually incorrect at the aggregate level. But it persists for five specific, data-traceable reasons. Each is correctable.

| Year | "Crime increased" | "About the same" |
|---|---|---|
| 2009 | 38% | 43% |
| 2014 | 30% | 48% |
| 2019 | 42% | 41% |

### Five causes of the perception gap — and the corrective for each

**1. The CSI aggregation conceals a violent-crime crossover.** Violent CSI has climbed since 2014; non-violent CSI has fallen (Figure 2). The aggregate drops because property crime's decline is larger in magnitude, but residents are responding to the component that matters more to personal safety: violent offences are getting worse. The single-number CSI buries this signal.

*Corrective:* Report violent and non-violent CSI separately in all provincial communications. A split presentation eliminates the aggregation artifact that fuels public skepticism.

**2. Rising crimes are visible; declining crimes are not.** Shoplifting rose 79 per 100,000 over five years (Figure 16). It happens where people shop, commute, and live. The crimes driving the decline — theft from vehicles (-772/100k), breaking and entering (-270/100k) — are non-events: cars not broken into, homes not entered (Figure 12). The public cannot observe what didn't happen. Their sample of crime is biased toward the rising categories by the nature of human perception.

*Corrective:* Publish a quarterly scorecard pairing rising and declining categories side by side. Lead with the ratio: "For every 1 additional shoplifting incident per 100,000, there were 10 fewer vehicle thefts."

**3. A 70% unsolved rate destroys trust in the data source.** Property crime clears below 30% (Figures 17-18). This rate has not improved in twenty years despite sustained budget increases. When the institution that produces the crime statistics cannot resolve 7 in 10 reported cases, the public has a rational basis for discounting its claim that crime is falling. The credibility problem is institutional, not informational.

*Corrective:* Implement closed-loop case reporting. Every filed report receives a status update within 30 days — resolution, referral, or explicit closure with reason. Silence after a report is the single most corrosive factor for public trust.

**4. Provincial averages erase locally high crime.** Chilliwack (11,352/100k) and Kamloops (10,546/100k) face per-capita rates 2x Vancouver's 5,438 (Figure 26). For these communities, the claim "crime is falling" does not match daily reality. The provincial GSS average combines their experience with lower-crime coastal populations, producing a number that misleads both groups.

*Corrective:* Publish jurisdiction-level crime dashboards. Allocate prevention and enforcement resources proportional to per-capita rates, not absolute volume. Interior communities systematically receive less than their crime burden warrants under current formulas.

**5. Visible disorder drives perception but falls outside the CSI.** Open drug use, street encampments, and aggressive panhandling are not Criminal Code offences. They do not appear in the Crime Severity Index. But in opioid-affected communities — concentrated in BC's interior — they dominate daily experience. The gap between what people see and what the statistics measure is structural: the CSI was not designed to capture disorder, and the public was never told this.

*Corrective:* Create a separate "community disorder index" drawn from police calls for service (wellness checks, trespass, mischief, bylaw complaints). Publish it alongside the CSI. This gives the public a metric that reflects what they observe and prevents conflation of disorder with Criminal Code trends.

### Low confidence and underreporting lock the gap in place

![Figure 32: Confidence in police by province — BC's middling confidence mirrors the national pattern](outputs/charts/q5_confidence_by_province.png)

Police confidence is moderate: 30% report "a great deal," 47% "some," 17% "not very much," 5% "none" (2019 GSS) — tracking the national distribution (32%/46%/15%/5%). Low confidence creates a feedback loop: residents who distrust police also distrust police-reported statistics, which reinforces their belief that the official picture is wrong.

![Figure 33: Reporting rates by crime type — from 6% (sexual assault) to 60% (motor vehicle theft)](outputs/charts/q5_reporting_rates.png)

Reporting rates range from 6% (sexual assault) to 60% (motor vehicle theft). Nationally, 71% of victimizations go unreported (2019 GSS). The crime statistics that the public is asked to trust are built on a foundation where nearly three-quarters of actual crime never enters the system. Until reporting improves, the gap between lived experience and official statistics will remain — not because the public is uninformed, but because the data itself is incomplete.
