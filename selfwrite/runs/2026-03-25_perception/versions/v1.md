## 5. The Public Thinks Crime Is Rising — the Data Disagrees

The data is unambiguous: BC's Crime Severity Index fell 46% from 2003 to 2014 and dropped another 7.4% in 2024. The public disagrees. In 2019, 42% of BC residents told the General Social Survey that crime in their neighbourhood had increased — up 12 percentage points from 30% in 2014.

| Year | "Crime increased" | "About the same" |
|---|---|---|
| 2009 | 38% | 43% |
| 2014 | 30% | 48% |
| 2019 | 42% | 41% |

![Figure 31: Perception vs. reality — 42% believe crime is rising while CSI remains below its peak](outputs/charts/q5_perception_vs_reality.png)

The public is wrong about the aggregate trend. But the gap has identifiable, correctable causes — each traceable to specific data in this analysis.

### Five causes of the gap

**1. The visible crimes are rising; the declining crimes are invisible.** Shoplifting rose 79 per 100,000 over five years (Figure 16). It happens where people shop, commute, and live. The crimes behind the decline — theft from vehicles (-772/100k), breaking and entering (-270/100k) — are non-events: cars not broken into, homes not burglarized (Figure 12). The public observes the increases and cannot observe the decreases. This is not a failure of public reasoning; it is a sampling problem.

*Corrective:* Publish a quarterly "crime change scorecard" that makes declining categories as visible as rising ones. Lead with the net: "For every 79 additional shoplifting incidents per 100,000, there were 772 fewer thefts from vehicles."

**2. Violent crime IS rising — the headline hides it.** Violent CSI has climbed since 2014 while non-violent CSI fell (Figure 2). The aggregate drops because property crime's decline is larger. Residents sensing crime "getting worse" are detecting a real signal — the compositional shift toward more serious offences — that the headline CSI conceals.

*Corrective:* Report violent and non-violent CSI separately in all public communications. The single-number CSI misleads when its components move in opposite directions.

**3. Low clearance erodes statistical credibility.** Property crime clears at below 30% — 7 in 10 cases unresolved (Figures 17-18). Twenty years of budget increases have not moved this rate. A resident who reports a theft and hears nothing back has no reason to trust the same institution's claim that crime is declining. The clearance gap is not just an enforcement failure; it is a credibility failure.

*Corrective:* Implement closed-loop reporting: every filed report receives a status update within 30 days, even if the update is "investigation concluded, no suspect identified." Silence after a report is the single most corrosive factor for public trust.

**4. Interior communities face genuinely high crime.** Chilliwack (11,352/100k) and Kamloops (10,546/100k) have per-capita rates roughly 2x Vancouver's 5,438 (Figure 26). For these residents, the perception matches their local reality. The provincial GSS average combines their experience with lower-crime coastal populations, producing a number that describes neither group accurately.

*Corrective:* Publish jurisdiction-level crime dashboards so residents see data for their own community, not a provincial aggregate. Fund crime-reduction efforts proportional to per-capita rates, not absolute volume.

**5. Visible disorder is counted as crime by residents but not by the CSI.** Open drug use, street-level encampments, and aggressive panhandling are not Criminal Code offences. They do not appear in the Crime Severity Index. But they dominate the daily experience of residents in opioid-affected communities. The gap between what people see and what the statistics measure is structural.

*Corrective:* Track and report disorder-related police calls (wellness checks, trespass, mischief) as a separate public metric. This gives the public a statistical reflection of what they observe and prevents conflation of disorder with Criminal Code offences.

### Confidence and reporting compound the problem

![Figure 32: Confidence in police by province — BC's middling confidence mirrors the national pattern](outputs/charts/q5_confidence_by_province.png)

Police confidence is moderate: 30% report "a great deal," 47% "some," 17% "not very much," 5% "none at all" (2019 GSS). These figures mirror the national distribution (32%/46%/15%/5%). Low confidence compounds the gap — residents who don't trust police don't trust police-produced statistics.

![Figure 33: Reporting rates by crime type — from 6% (sexual assault) to 60% (motor vehicle theft)](outputs/charts/q5_reporting_rates.png)

Reporting rates range from 6% (sexual assault) to 60% (motor vehicle theft). Nationally, 71% of victimizations go unreported (2019 GSS). The statistical record captures a filtered subset of crime. Victims of unreported offences know their experience is real regardless of whether it reaches the data. Until the reporting gap narrows, the perception gap will persist.
