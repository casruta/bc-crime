## 5. The Public Thinks Crime Is Rising — the Data Disagrees

BC's Crime Severity Index fell 46% from 2003 to 2014 and dropped another 7.4% in 2024. The public disagrees: 42% of BC residents said crime had increased in their neighbourhood (2019 GSS), up 12 points from 30% in 2014. The public is wrong about the aggregate. But the gap has five identifiable, correctable causes.

| Year | "Crime increased" | "About the same" |
|---|---|---|
| 2009 | 38% | 43% |
| 2014 | 30% | 48% |
| 2019 | 42% | 41% |

![Figure 31: Perception vs. reality — 42% believe crime is rising while CSI remains below its peak](outputs/charts/q5_perception_vs_reality.png)

### Why perception diverges from the data

**1. The headline number hides a violent-crime crossover.** Violent CSI has climbed since 2014; non-violent CSI has fallen (Figure 2). The aggregate drops because property crime's decline is larger, but the public intuits the compositional shift toward more serious offences. Residents who say "crime feels worse" are detecting a real signal that the single-number CSI conceals.

*Action:* Report violent and non-violent CSI separately. The single-number index misleads when its components move in opposite directions.

**2. The crimes people see are rising; the declining crimes are invisible.** Shoplifting rose 79 per 100,000 (Figure 16) — visible in stores, pharmacies, and transit. Theft from vehicles fell 772 per 100,000 and breaking and entering fell 270 (Figure 12) — non-events no one observes. The public samples from what it witnesses, and that sample is biased toward the rising categories.

*Action:* Publish a quarterly scorecard that pairs rising and declining categories. Lead with the net: "For every 79 additional shoplifting incidents per 100,000, there were 772 fewer vehicle thefts."

**3. Unresolved cases destroy statistical credibility.** Property crime clears below 30% — 7 in 10 cases go unresolved (Figures 17-18). This rate has not improved in twenty years despite sustained budget growth. A resident who reports a theft and receives no follow-up has no basis for trusting the same institution's claim that crime is falling.

*Action:* Implement closed-loop reporting: every filed report receives a status update within 30 days. Silence after a report is the most corrosive factor for public trust in crime statistics.

**4. Provincial averages mask locally high crime.** Chilliwack (11,352/100k) and Kamloops (10,546/100k) face per-capita rates 2x Vancouver's 5,438 (Figure 26). For residents of these communities, the perception matches their reality. The provincial GSS average combines their experience with lower-crime coastal populations, producing a number that describes neither group.

*Action:* Publish jurisdiction-level dashboards so communities see their own data. Allocate crime-reduction resources proportional to per-capita rates, not absolute volume.

**5. Visible disorder falls outside the statistics.** Open drug use, encampments, and aggressive panhandling are not Criminal Code offences. They don't appear in the CSI. But they dominate daily experience in opioid-affected communities. The gap between what people see and what the data measures is structural — no amount of CSI publication will bridge it.

*Action:* Track disorder-related police calls (wellness checks, trespass, mischief) as a separate public metric. This gives the public a statistical reflection of what they observe and prevents conflation with Criminal Code offences.

### Confidence and reporting widen the gap

![Figure 32: Confidence in police by province — BC's middling confidence mirrors the national pattern](outputs/charts/q5_confidence_by_province.png)

Police confidence is moderate: 30% report "a great deal," 47% "some," 17% "not very much," 5% "none" (2019 GSS) — mirroring the national distribution (32%/46%/15%/5%). Residents who don't trust police don't trust police-produced statistics.

![Figure 33: Reporting rates by crime type — from 6% (sexual assault) to 60% (motor vehicle theft)](outputs/charts/q5_reporting_rates.png)

Reporting rates range from 6% (sexual assault) to 60% (motor vehicle theft). Nationally, 71% of victimizations go unreported. The statistical record captures a filtered subset of crime. Until reporting improves, the gap between lived experience and official data will persist — and so will public distrust of any statistic derived from police reports alone.
